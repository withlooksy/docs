## What makes a credible Looksy case study?

A useful case study shows what changed, how it was measured, and what else could have affected the result. It should not present an illustrative scenario as a real merchant result.

## Evidence to include

- test dates and store context;
- products and traffic included;
- baseline and comparison method;
- try-on usage and completion signals;
- conversion and return-rate calculations;
- sample sizes;
- promotions, inventory changes, or seasonal effects;
- limitations and uncertainty.

## Recommended case-study structure

### 1. Starting point

Describe the shopper problem and the baseline metrics before Looksy was introduced.

### 2. Implementation

Record where Looksy appeared, which products were included, and how long the test ran.

### 3. Measurement

Explain whether the comparison used an A/B test, matched products, or a before-and-after period. State why that method was appropriate.

### 4. Results

Report actual counts alongside percentages. A percentage without sessions, orders, or returns can be misleading.

### 5. Limitations

Identify changes in traffic, pricing, promotions, product mix, stock, and return windows.

### 6. Next action

State whether the evidence supports expanding, refining, or ending the test.

<Note>
  Merchant outcomes vary. Looksy does not guarantee a specific conversion lift, return-rate reduction, or ROI.
</Note>
